Prompt Engineering Reference

The 5 Core Elements of an Effective Prompt

Structure eliminates guesswork. Order determines how the model weights every token that follows.

TL;DR

LLMs have finite context windows, and instruction files are loaded on every turn. Too much content causes attention dilution (rules get ignored), token waste, instruction conflicts, and leaves less room for actual work. Keep prompts tight and structured — only include what the model cannot infer from context alone.

When you do write a prompt, front-load your framing. Earlier tokens carry more interpretive weight. Put your most important information first.

The 5 Core Elements

1
Role / Context
Who is the AI right now, and what situation is it operating in?
Anchors the model — narrows its trillions of parameters to the tone, knowledge domain, and vocabulary relevant to the persona. Everything the model reads after this is interpreted through this lens.
Weak
(No identity given)
Strong
"You are a senior content marketing manager specializing in B2B SaaS."
2
Task
What exactly needs to get done?
Defines the action, scope, and specific details of the request. Be as explicit as possible — vague commands force the model to average across its training data.
Weak
"Write a blog post."
Strong
"Draft a 500-word blog post explaining how AI is streamlining supply chain logistics."
3
Input
What material does the task operate on?
The raw data, code, text, or background information the model needs to work with. Placed here — after role and task are set — so the model reads it with purpose rather than neutrally.
Weak
"Make it sound professional." (no audience given)
Strong
"Our audience is supply chain executives at Fortune 500 companies who are skeptical of new technology but care deeply about cost reduction."
4
Constraints
What should the AI avoid doing?
The most frequently skipped, yet most valuable element. Telling a model what not to do is often more powerful than telling it what to do. Applied here — to a solution the model is already forming — constraints are more binding than if placed earlier.
Weak
(No constraints given)
Strong
"Do not use jargon like 'synergy' or 'paradigm shift'. Do not mention competitors."
5
Output Format
What should the physical result look like?
If you want a table, ask for a table. If you don't specify the format, the model guesses — and defaults to a wall of text. Placed last because it only matters once the model knows what it's producing.
Weak
"Give me the results."
Strong
"Format the output as a Markdown table with three columns: Concept, Definition, and Real-World Example."

Why Order Matters: The Outside-In Principle

Order Element Why it goes here
1 Role / Context Establishes who the AI is before it reads anything else. Every subsequent token is weighted against this frame.
2 Task States the goal while the role is fresh — the model now scans everything that follows with purpose, not neutrally.
3 Input Background material is interpreted through the combined lens of role + task simultaneously, not in isolation.
4 Constraints Applied to a solution the model is already forming, making guardrails more binding than if placed earlier.
5 Output Format A final narrowing at the moment of generation — collapses the solution space last, not first.

The logic is outside-in: each layer narrows the model's interpretation space before the next one adds detail. LLMs generate token by token — each influenced by all prior tokens. Front-loading the right context means every word of your prompt is processed under the correct framing.

The Research: "Lost in the Middle"

Liu et al. (2023) — Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics — arXiv:2307.03172

LLMs do not read prompts linearly — they weight tokens by position. This landmark study (Stanford, UC Berkeley, Samaya AI) tested multiple frontier models on multi-document question answering and found a striking pattern:

15–25% accuracy drop for
middle-positioned content
U-shaped performance curve
across context positions
Start & End positions maintain
highest reliability
Accuracy vs. position of critical information — the "U-shaped performance curve"

The root cause is structural: transformer attention uses Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE), which causes attention scores to decay naturally for tokens far apart in the sequence. Initial tokens accumulate attention influence across every subsequent layer — a phenomenon called attention sinks.

Earlier tokens carry disproportionately more interpretive weight. Put your most important framing — Role and Task — at the very top.

Source: Liu et al., "Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts." Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics, 2024.   arXiv:2307.03172 ↗
